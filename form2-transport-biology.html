Introduction

What is Transport?

Definition
Transport is the movement of substances from one part of the body to another.
🎯
By the end of this section, you should be able to: define transport, explain why it is necessary, and distinguish transport in plants vs animals.

Why is Transport Necessary?

🍎
Nutrients
Move nutrients from one point to another in the body
💨
Respiratory Gases
Movement of O₂ and CO₂ for respiration
🧪
Waste Removal
Elimination of metabolic waste products
⚗️
Hormones & Enzymes
Movement of hormones, enzymes, water, and salts
Transport ensures all cells receive what they need and have waste products removed, enabling life functions.

Transport in Plants

Root Hair: Structure & Function

Root Hair Structure
Root hairs are cells near the root tip with elongated finger-like projections in contact with soil particles. They are permeable to water and mineral salts.

Adaptations of Root Hairs

  • Long, narrow & numerous — increases surface area for absorption
  • Many mitochondria — supply energy for active transport of mineral salts
  • Thin-walled — speeds up rate of absorption of water and mineral salts
Always state WHY the adaptation helps — e.g. "long to increase surface area for absorption."

Transport Structures

Xylem & Phloem

🟡 XYLEM
Function

Transports water and mineral salts from soil to leaves (upward flow)

Adaptations
  • Lignified/thickened — prevents collapsing
  • Narrow — facilitates capillarity
  • No cross walls — continuous flow of water column
  • Bordered pits — lateral movement of water
  • Dead cells — hollow tubes, no resistance
🟢 PHLOEM
Function

Translocates manufactured food from leaves to other parts (bidirectional)

Components
  • Sieve tubes — conduct food; have cross walls (sieve plates)
  • Companion cells — supply nutrients & energy for translocation; regulate tube cells
  • Cytoplasm — present (unlike xylem)

Water Movement

Forces Moving Water Through Plants

🌊
Osmosis
Water moves through semi-permeable membranes of root hairs and between cells from regions of high water potential to low
⬆️
Root Pressure
A force that pushes water up the stem from roots. Causes guttation — exudation of water from leaf tips
🔬
Capillarity
Due to narrowness of xylem vessels — water rises up narrow tubes against gravity
🔗
Cohesion
Attraction of water molecules to each other — keeps the water column unbroken
🧲
Adhesion
Attraction of water molecules to xylem walls — helps pull water upward
💨
Transpiration Pull
Main force! Water vapour evaporates from spongy mesophyll creating suction that pulls water from roots up to leaves
Transpiration pull is the most important force moving water in tall plants. "CATCH" = Cohesion, Adhesion, Transpiration pull, Capillarity, root pressure, Osmosis.
Transpiration

Transpiration: Definition & Importance

Definition
Transpiration is the loss of water from a plant to the atmosphere.
🌡️
Sites of Transpiration
  • Stomata — stomatal transpiration (main)
  • Lenticels — lenticular transpiration
  • Cuticle — cuticular transpiration
✅
Importance
  • Cooling the plant
  • Transport of water upward
  • Transport of mineral salts
  • Excretion of excess water
⚠️
Excess Transpiration
Excess transpiration causes wilting — plants lose turgidity and droop. Drooping reduces surface area exposed to sun, reducing cuticular transpiration.
When asked to "state the importance of transpiration," always include: cooling, water transport, mineral salt transport, and excretion of excess water.
Transpiration

Factors Affecting Rate of Transpiration

🏗️ Structural Factors

  • Number of stomata — more stomata = higher rate
  • Guard cell turgidity — open stomata = high rate; closed = low rate
  • Leaf size — larger surface area = higher rate
  • Leaf fall — reduces rate
  • Cuticle thickness — thin/absent cuticle = higher rate

🌍 Environmental Factors

  • Temperature — high temp = high rate (more evaporation)
  • Humidity — high humidity = low rate; low humidity = high rate
  • Wind — moving air = higher rate than still air
  • Light intensity — high light = more evaporation = higher rate
  • Soil water — more available = more absorbed = more lost
  • Atmospheric pressure — high pressure = more evaporation
↑ Light → ↑ Rate ↑ Humidity → ↓ Rate ↑ Temperature → ↑ Rate Wind → ↑ Rate ↑ Stomata → ↑ Rate
Adaptations

Plant Adaptations to Water Loss

🌿
Mesophytes
Grow in soils with adequate water
  • Water loss balanced by absorption
  • No special adaptations needed
🌵
Xerophytes
Grow in dry conditions
  • Deep roots to absorb water
  • Succulent/fleshy leaves to store water
  • Few, sunken stomata
  • Thick waxy cuticle
  • Hairy, folding leaves
  • Needle-like leaves/spines
  • Small, reduced leaf surface
🌊
Hydrophytes
Grow in water
  • Broad leaves
  • Stomata on upper surface only
  • Absence or reduced cuticle
  • Large air spaces (aerenchyma)
  • Reduced vascular bundles
  • Sclereids for support

General Adaptations to Reduce Water Loss

Thick waxy cuticle Reduced leaf size Sunken stomata Shedding of leaves Rolling of leaves

Translocation Experiments

Proving Phloem Translocates Food

💍
1. Ringing Experiment
  • Cut a ring in the bark (including phloem) of a woody plant
  • Observe for 3+ weeks
  • Swelling appears above ring — food accumulates from leaves above
  • Control: phloem intact → no swelling
☢️
2. Radioactive Tracers
  • Plant exposed to radioactive carbon C-14
  • C-14 found in end products of photosynthesis
  • Finally detected in phloem
  • C-14 moves in both directions in phloem
🦟
3. Aphid Stylets
  • Aphids feed on plant phloem using stylets (mouthparts)
  • Stylets dissected, exudate collected
  • Sucrose found as major component
  • Proves phloem translocates manufactured food
When asked to describe the ringing experiment, include: what you do (cut ring), what you observe (swelling above ring), and what it proves (phloem carries food downward).

Xylem Transport Experiment (Dye Test)

Cut a stem under water → place in eosin/red ink dye → leave 20–60 min → cut thin section → mount on slide → examine under microscope. Dye appears only in xylem vessels — proving xylem transports water.
Mineral Salts

Uptake of Mineral Salts by Plants

Why Plants Need Mineral Salts
Plants require mineral salts for metabolism and proper functioning of their bodies. Mineral salts are taken from the soil into root hairs in the form of solution.
⚡
Active Transport (Main Process)
Mineral salts are absorbed by active transport — requiring energy (ATP) from respiration.

Involves carrier molecules that pick up mineral ions and transport them against the concentration gradient (from low to high concentration).

Minerals then carried with water to stems and leaves.
↔️
Diffusion (Secondary Process)
Mineral salts also enter by diffusion due to the concentration gradient between mineral ions in the soil solution and those in the root hair cell sap.

Works when mineral concentration in soil is higher than in root hair cell — passive, no energy needed.

Forces Moving Minerals Through the Plant

ForceHow It WorksEnergy Needed?
Active TransportCarrier proteins move ions against concentration gradient; uses ATPYes (from respiration)
DiffusionIons move from high concentration in soil to low concentration in rootNo (passive)
Mass flowMinerals dissolved in water and carried along with water movement in xylemNo (passive)
Key point: The MAIN process for mineral salt absorption is active transport. Diffusion is secondary. Active transport requires ATP — hence root hairs have many mitochondria.
🔬 How to Demonstrate Xylem Transports Minerals
Place a plant in a solution containing radioactive phosphorus. The radioactive phosphorus is later detected in the leaves, proving xylem transported it from roots to leaves.
Stomata

Opening & Closing of Stomata

Structure of Stomata
Minute pores found in leaf epidermis. Each consists of a slit-like opening bordered by two large, bean-shaped guard cells. Guard cells contain chloroplasts (unlike other epidermal cells). Inner walls of guard cells are thicker than outer walls.

Factors Affecting Stomatal Opening

  • Water — low water: stomata close; high water: stomata open
  • Light — stomata open in bright light; close in darkness
  • Temperature — affects metabolic rate of guard cells
  • CO₂ concentration — high CO₂ → stomata close

Theories of Stomatal Movement

  • Interconversion of starch and sugar
  • pH theory
  • Mineral ion (potassium ion) concentration theory

Mechanism — Day vs Night

☀️ During DAY — Stomata OPEN
  1. Photosynthesis uses CO₂ → CO₂ concentration falls
  2. pH rises in guard cells
  3. Starch converted to sugar → osmotic pressure rises
  4. Water enters guard cells by osmosis → cells become turgid
  5. Thick inner walls bow → stomata open
  6. Potassium ions accumulate → raise osmotic pressure → stomata open
🌙 During NIGHT — Stomata CLOSE
  1. No photosynthesis → CO₂ accumulates
  2. CO₂ + H₂O → carbonic acid → pH drops
  3. Sugar converted to starch → osmotic pressure falls
  4. Water leaves guard cells by osmosis → cells become flaccid
  5. Stomata close (conserves water)
Advantage of day opening / night closing: Day — allows CO₂ in for photosynthesis, O₂ out, transpiration for water transport. Night — conserves water when photosynthesis is not occurring.

The Mammalian Heart

Heart Adaptations

💪 Cardiac Muscle
Made of muscles that contract and relax synchronously without requiring nervous stimulation (myogenic). Nerve supply controls strength and frequency only.
🔀 Four Chambers
Right and left sides completely separated. Prevents mixing of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood. Allows efficient double circulation.
🚪 Valves
Tricuspid valve: between right atrium and right ventricle
Bicuspid (mitral): between left atrium and left ventricle
Prevent backflow of blood
🏋️ Thick Left Ventricle
Left ventricle has thickest walls — pumps blood throughout the whole body (systemic circulation) against high resistance. Right ventricle only pumps to lungs (shorter distance).
⚡ Pacemaker (SA Node)
A pacemaker in the heart muscle initiates and synchronises contractions automatically, maintaining a regular heartbeat.
🩸 Coronary Vessels
Heart muscle is supplied by coronary arteries and drained by coronary veins — ensures the heart itself receives oxygen and nutrients.
Why is blood leaving lungs sometimes not fully oxygenated? Poor ventilation of lungs, blockage of alveoli, or high cardiac frequency (blood moves too fast).

Artery Adaptations
Thick muscular walls + elastic — withstand high pressure from heartbeat. Narrow lumen maintains high pressure.
Capillary Adaptations
One cell thick — allows diffusion of substances (O₂, glucose) to tissues. Intercellular spaces allow leucocytes to pass.
Vein Adaptations
Valves prevent backflow. Wide lumen — low resistance. Thin walls — blood at low pressure.

Why is Blood Pressure Greater in Arterioles than Veins?

  • Blood is pumped to arteries by the heart at high pressure
  • Blood pressure in veins is reduced by capillary resistance
  • Arteries have narrow lumen which maintains high pressure; veins have wide lumen which reduces pressure
  • Arteries have thicker muscular walls which generate pressure; veins have thinner walls

Common Heart Diseases

Thrombosis Atheroma (plaque build-up) Arteriosclerosis Varicose veins Cerebral vascular thrombosis
Arterioles vs veins pressure: arteries = high (near heart, narrow, muscular); veins = low (far, wide, thin walls).
Blood

Blood Composition & Functions

🟡 Plasma (~55%)
  • Transport of glucose, amino acids, fatty acids, glycerol from small intestine to liver and tissues
  • Transport of hormones and enzymes from glands to target tissues
  • Transport of CO₂ to lungs; urea to kidneys
  • Distribution of heat
  • Bathes tissues — exchange of materials
  • Contains fibrinogen and prothrombin for blood clotting
🔴 Red Blood Cells (Erythrocytes)
  • Transport O₂ (as oxyhaemoglobin)
  • Transport CO₂ as bicarbonates
⚪ White Blood Cells (Leucocytes)
  • Engulf foreign bodies (phagocytosis)
  • Produce antibodies
  • Produce antitoxins — neutralize bacterial toxins
🟣 Blood Platelets (Thrombocytes)
  • Produce thrombokinase/thromboplastin for clotting
  • Prevent loss of blood, water, and mineral salts
Blood Cells

Red Blood Cells: Adaptations

Adaptations of RBCs

  • Many per unit volume — carry more O₂ and CO₂
  • Biconcave shape — large surface area for O₂ and CO₂ absorption
  • No nucleus — more space for haemoglobin
  • Flexible/alter shape — squeeze through narrow capillaries
  • Haemoglobin with high affinity for O₂ and CO₂

Blood Functions

Transport of Oxygen & CO₂

🌬️ OXYGEN Transport
  • O₂ concentration higher in alveoli than blood
  • O₂ dissolves in moisture of alveoli → diffuses through thin epithelial and capillary walls into plasma and RBCs
  • O₂ + haemoglobin → oxyhaemoglobin
  • Blood becomes oxygenated
  • Oxyhaemoglobin breaks down at tissues → releases O₂ for respiration
  • Haemoglobin returns to lungs for more O₂
💨 CO₂ Transport
  • CO₂ diffuses from cells into blood plasma and RBCs
  • CO₂ + H₂O → carbonic acid → hydrogen carbonate (main method, in RBCs)
  • Enzyme carbonic anhydrase in RBCs speeds up this reaction
  • Hydrogen carbonate carried in blood to lungs
  • In lungs: hydrogen carbonate → CO₂ → diffuses into alveolar cavity
  • CO₂ expelled during expiration
Advantage of CO₂ transport in RBCs (not plasma)
• pH of blood not altered → homeostasis maintained
• Carbonic anhydrase in RBCs enables fast loading and offloading of CO₂
Blood Clotting

Blood Clotting Process

Definition
Blood clotting is the process in which blood components clump together to prevent loss of blood from an injured/cut vessel.

Substances Involved

  • Protein: fibrinogen / prothrombin
  • Vitamin: K
  • Enzyme: thrombokinase / thromboplastin
  • Mineral: calcium ions (Ca²⁺)

Roles of Clotting

  • Prevents loss of blood and body fluids
  • Conserves water and salts
  • Prevents entry of microorganisms
  • Regulates body temperature
  • Enables wound to heal faster

Why doesn't blood in vessels clot? → Presence of anticoagulant (e.g. heparin) in blood

Defence

Immunity & Allergic Reactions

Definition
Immunity is the resistance to disease by organisms.
🧬
Natural (Innate) Immunity
Inherited/transmitted from parent to offspring. Inborn/innate immunity present from birth without prior exposure to disease.
💉
Acquired Immunity
Developed after suffering from a disease or through vaccination. Body produces antibodies in response to antigens.

Vaccination

  • Protects body against infectious diseases
  • Prevents spread/transmission of certain diseases
  • Diseases vaccinated against: tuberculosis, poliomyelitis, measles, whooping cough, diphtheria

Allergic Reactions

What is an Allergy?
Excessive sensitivity and reaction of an individual to certain environmental substances (e.g. dust, pollen, perfumes, smoke).

How it occurs: Substances act as antigens → antigen-antibody reaction on cell surfaces → cells release histamine → causes irritation, itching, nasal discharge.
Blood Transfusion

Blood Transfusion & Related Issues

Definition
Blood transfusion is the introduction of blood from one person to another.
When Needed
  • During accidents
  • During surgery
  • Bleeding mothers giving birth
Restoring Blood Volume
  • Blood transfusion
  • Taking fluids orally
  • Eating iron-rich food
  • Taking iron tablets
Excessive Bleeding → Death
  • Anaemia / low blood volume
  • Loss of iron
  • Low RBC count / low Hb
  • Low O₂ → cell death
  • Loss of nutrients, dehydration

Precautions Before Transfusion

  • Blood must be disease-free
  • Sterilized equipment must be used
  • Blood of recipient and donor must be compatible — both ABO and Rhesus factor
  • Fresh blood must be used

Exam Preparation

KCSE Past Paper Questions

KCSE Q1 — Define transpiration. State three sites through which it occurs. (4 marks) ▼
Transpiration is the loss of water from a plant to the atmosphere. (1 mk)
Sites: (i) Stomata — stomatal transpiration (ii) Lenticels — lenticular transpiration (iii) Cuticle — cuticular transpiration (1 mk each = 3 mks)
KCSE Q2 — State five ways root hairs are adapted to their functions. (5 marks) ▼
1. Long/narrow/numerous — increase surface area for absorption
2. Many mitochondria — provide energy for active transport of mineral salts
3. Thin-walled — speeds up rate of absorption
4. Permeable to water and mineral salts — facilitates absorption
5. Single cell layer — reduces distance of diffusion
KCSE Q3 — Describe how blood clotting occurs. (5 marks) ▼
Damaged tissue/platelets release enzyme thromboplastin/thrombokinase. Thromboplastin converts prothrombin (plasma protein) to thrombin in presence of calcium ions. Thrombin converts fibrinogen (another plasma protein) to fibrin in presence of Vitamin K. Fibrin is insoluble and forms fibres that create a meshwork/clot over the wound.
KCSE Q4 — State four differences between xylem vessels and sieve tubes. (4 marks) ▼
1. Sieve tubes have cross walls; xylem vessels do not.
2. Xylem vessels are lignified; sieve tubes are not.
3. Sieve tubes have cytoplasm; xylem vessels do not (dead cells).
4. Xylem transports water/mineral salts; phloem translocates manufactured food.
KCSE Q5 — Explain how the mammalian heart is adapted to its functions. (6 marks) ▼
1. Made of cardiac muscle — contracts/relaxes synchronously without nervous stimulation (myogenic).
2. Four chambers — prevents mixing of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood.
3. Valves (tricuspid, bicuspid) — prevent backflow of blood.
4. Left ventricle has thickest walls — generates enough pressure to pump blood to the whole body.
5. Pacemaker (SA node) — initiates and synchronizes contractions.
6. Coronary arteries supply heart muscle with oxygen and nutrients.
KCSE Q6 — Describe an experiment to show phloem translocates food. (6 marks) ▼
Ringing experiment: Cut a ring in the bark (including phloem) of a woody plant. Observe daily for more than 3 weeks. A swelling appears above the ring due to accumulation of food from leaves (food cannot move past the ring). A second plant with phloem left intact shows no swelling. This proves phloem transports manufactured food downward.
KCSE Q7 — Explain why blood pressure is greater in arteries than veins. (4 marks) ▼
1. Blood is pumped from the heart to arteries at high pressure.
2. Arteries have narrow lumen which maintains high pressure; veins have wide lumen which reduces pressure.
3. Blood pressure in veins reduced by capillary resistance.
4. Arteries have thick muscular walls that generate pressure; veins have thin walls.
KCSE Q8 — State ways red blood cells are adapted to their functions. (5 marks) ▼
1. Many per unit volume — carry more O₂ and CO₂.
2. Biconcave shape — large surface area for absorption of O₂ and CO₂.
3. No nucleus — more space for haemoglobin.
4. Can alter shape — pass through narrow capillary lumen.
5. Haemoglobin has high affinity for O₂ — efficient uptake and release.
KCSE Q9 — Distinguish between natural and acquired immunity. (2 marks) ▼
Natural immunity: Inherited/transmitted from parent to offspring; inborn/innate; present from birth.
Acquired immunity: Developed after suffering from a disease or through vaccination; produced as a response to antigens.
KCSE Q10 — Explain the forces involved in movement of water from soil to leaves. (8 marks) ▼
1. Osmosis — water moves from soil through root hair cell membrane due to higher concentration of cell sap.
2. Root pressure — force pushes water from root cells into xylem and up the stem.
3. Capillarity — narrowness of xylem vessels causes water to rise.
4. Cohesion — water molecules attract each other maintaining continuous water column.
5. Adhesion — water molecules adhere to xylem walls, pulling water upward.
6. Transpiration pull — water evaporates from spongy mesophyll creating suction that pulls water from roots up.
7. Active transport — minerals and some water transported across endodermis against concentration gradient.
8. Diffusion — minerals taken up by diffusion along concentration gradient.
