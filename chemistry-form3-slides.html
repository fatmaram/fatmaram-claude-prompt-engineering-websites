📖 Key Definitions — At a Glance

ATOM

The smallest particle of an element that takes part in a chemical reaction. Made of protons, neutrons, and electrons.

NUCLEUS

The central core of an atom, containing protons and neutrons. Very dense and positively charged.

ISOTOPE

Atoms of the same element with the same atomic number but different mass numbers (different neutrons).

ION

Formed when an atom gains or loses electrons to become stable — either cation (+) or anion (−).

Subatomic Particles

Every atom is built from three fundamental particles, each with a distinct role and location.

⊕
PROTON
Charge: Positive (+1)
Location: Nucleus (centre)
Relative mass: 1
Key fact: Number of protons = Atomic Number (Z)
◎
NEUTRON
Charge: None (neutral)
Location: Nucleus (centre)
Relative mass: 1
Key fact: Protons + Neutrons = Mass Number (A)
⊖
ELECTRON
Charge: Negative (−1)
Location: Energy levels / orbitals
Relative mass: 1/1840 ≈ negligible
Key fact: In neutral atom, electrons = protons

Isotopes & Atomic Notation

Standard Atomic Notation

By convention, the mass number is written as a superscript and the atomic number as a subscript to the left of the chemical symbol:

A Z X
Example: Carbon-12 → ¹²₆C  →  6 protons, 6 neutrons, 6 electrons
DEFINITION

What are Isotopes?

Isotopes are atoms of the same element having the same atomic number (same protons) but a different mass number (different neutrons). They have identical chemical properties but different physical properties.

Relative Atomic Mass (RAM)

DEFINITION

What is RAM?

The Relative Atomic Mass (RAM) is the mass of an average atom of an element compared to 1/12 of one atom of ¹²C, whose mass is arbitrarily fixed as 12.000 atomic mass units (a.m.u).

RAM = Σ [(abundance % ÷ 100) × mass number]
⚠️ Key Rules
  • ✓ RAM has no units
  • ✓ RAM is always closer to the most abundant isotope
  • ✓ Determined using a mass spectrometer
📊 From Mass Spectrometer

The mass spectrometer determines each isotope and its relative abundance. These abundances are then used in the RAM calculation.

Electronic Configuration

DEFINITION

Energy Levels / Orbitals

An energy level is a fixed region surrounding the nucleus occupied by electrons of the same potential energy. Numbered 1, 2, 3, 4… outward from the nucleus. The arrangement of electrons across levels is called the electronic configuration.

🎯 How to Find Period & Group from Configuration

PERIOD

Count the number of occupied energy levels = Period number

Na = 2:8:1 → 3 levels → Period 3
K = 2:8:8:1 → 4 levels → Period 4
GROUP

Count the outer energy level electrons = Group number

Na = 2:8:1 → 1 outer e⁻ → Group I
Cl = 2:8:7 → 7 outer e⁻ → Group VII

The Periodic Table

DEFINITION

A periodic table is a horizontal and vertical arrangement of elements according to their atomic numbers. Arranged in 1913 by British scientist Henry Moseley, building on earlier work by Russian scientist Dmitri Mendeleev.

↔
PERIODS (Horizontal)

7 periods total · Same number of occupied energy levels · Named 1, 2, 3…

↕
GROUPS (Vertical)

18 groups total · Same number of outer electrons · Named I, II, III…

Stability, Ions & Valency

STABLE ATOM

An atom with a full outer energy level is stable. Group 8/18 elements (noble gases) are all stable — outer level is complete.

UNSTABLE ATOM

All other atoms are unstable and react to achieve stability by gaining or losing outer electrons, forming ions.

CATION (+ion)

Elements with <4 outer e⁻ lose/donate electrons → positively charged ion. Generally metals form cations.

Na → Na⁺ + e⁻
Al → Al³⁺ + 3e⁻
ANION (−ion)

Elements with >4 outer e⁻ gain/acquire electrons → negatively charged ion. Generally non-metals form anions.

F + e⁻ → F⁻
O + 2e⁻ → O²⁻

Elements in the same group form a chemical family with similar properties that follow predictable trends going down the group.

Why these trends?

More energy levels → bigger atom → outer electron farther from nucleus → weaker nuclear attraction on outer electron → easier to lose → more reactive. Weaker metallic bonds also cause lower melting points.

Reaction with Water (reactivity ↑ down group)
2Li(s) + 2H₂O(l) → 2LiOH(aq) + H₂(g) — moderately vigorous; Li floats & fizzes
2Na(s) + 2H₂O(l) → 2NaOH(aq) + H₂(g) — very vigorous; Na melts into a ball
2K(s) + 2H₂O(l) → 2KOH(aq) + H₂(g) — explosive! K bursts into lilac flame
Burning in Air/Oxygen
4Li(s) + O₂(g) → 2Li₂O(s) — crimson flame
4Na(s) + O₂(g) → 2Na₂O(s) — yellow flame
4K(s) + O₂(g) → 2K₂O(s) — lilac/purple flame
Reaction with Chlorine
2Li(s) + Cl₂(g) → 2LiCl(s)
2Na(s) + Cl₂(g) → 2NaCl(s) — burns with yellow flame, white solid
2K(s) + Cl₂(g) → 2KCl(s) — Reactivity increases ↓ group
🔑 Uses of Alkali Metals
  • Sodium → making NaCN (extracts gold) · NaOH for detergents · reducing agent for Ti extraction
  • Sodium chloride → seasoning food
  • Molten Na/K mixture → coolant in nuclear reactors
  • Lithium → high-strength glass · dry cells (phones/laptops)

Group I vs Group II

Group II uses 2 delocalized electrons in metallic bonds vs Group I's 1 → stronger bonds → harder, higher melting point, better conductor. BUT smaller atomic radius than Group I (same period) → less electropositive.

Reactions with Water
Mg(s) + 2H₂O(l) → Mg(OH)₂(aq) + H₂(g) — very slow with cold water (bubbles cling to ribbon)
Mg(s) + H₂O(g) → MgO(s) + H₂(g) — burns vigorously in steam!
Ca(s) + 2H₂O(l) → Ca(OH)₂(aq) + H₂(g) — moderate, steady effervescence
Burning in Air
2Mg(s) + O₂(g) → 2MgO(s) — bright blinding white flame!
3Mg(s) + N₂(g) → Mg₃N₂(s) — also reacts with nitrogen in air
2Ca(s) + O₂(g) → 2CaO(s) — faint orange-red flame
Reactions with Dilute Acids
Mg(s) + 2HCl(aq) → MgCl₂(aq) + H₂(g)
Mg(s) + H₂SO₄(aq) → MgSO₄(aq) + H₂(g)
Ca(s) + H₂SO₄(aq) → CaSO₄(s) + H₂(g) [CaSO₄ coats Ca, slowing reaction]
🔑 Uses of Alkaline Earth Metals
  • Mg(OH)₂ → antacid medicine (relieves stomach acidity)
  • Duralumin (Mg + Al alloy) → aeroplane bodies (light & strong)
  • CaSO₄ (Plaster of Paris) → setting broken bones in hospitals
  • CaCO₃ → cement production; added to blast furnace
  • CaO (quicklime) → neutralizes acidic soils in farming
  • Ca(NO₃)₂ → nitrogenous fertilizer

⚡ Key Difference — Ionic Radius

Halogen ionic radius > atomic radius. When halogens gain an electron, the extra electron-electron repulsion expands the outer shell, and the nuclear attraction on the new electron decreases — making the ion larger than the atom.

Why unreactive?

The stable full outer energy level gives noble gases a very high 1st ionization energy — it takes enormous energy to remove an electron from a stable configuration. As atomic radius increases down the group, the 1st IE decreases, allowing very electronegative elements (O, F) to react with lower noble gases (e.g. XeF₆).

🔑 Uses of Noble Gases
  • Argon → light bulbs (inert atmosphere prevents filament oxidation) · arc welding (insulator)
  • Neon → street & advertisement lighting
  • Helium → weather balloons (safer than H₂, won't ignite) · deep-sea diving (mixed with O₂) · low-temperature thermometers

Key Reactions Summary

Ion Formation — Oxidation & Reduction

OXIDATION — lose electrons → cation
Na → Na⁺ + e⁻ (loses 1 e⁻)
Mg → Mg²⁺ + 2e⁻ (loses 2 e⁻)
Al → Al³⁺ + 3e⁻ (loses 3 e⁻)
REDUCTION — gain electrons → anion
F + e⁻ → F⁻ (gains 1 e⁻)
O + 2e⁻ → O²⁻ (gains 2 e⁻)
N + 3e⁻ → N³⁻ (gains 3 e⁻)
